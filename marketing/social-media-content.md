DRILL 1: RAINBOW CATCHES
Players partner up 15 feet apart. One tosses a high arc (rainbow). The other catches above their head with two hands.

WHY: Teaches fly ball basics without the fear. The soft toss builds confidence.

**Slide 3:**
DRILL 2: GROUND BALL ALLEY
Set up two cones 6 feet apart. Roll ground balls between the cones. Player fields and throws to first base.

WHY: Repetition on the most common play they'll see in a game. Builds muscle memory.

**Slide 4:**
DRILL 3: FOUR CORNERS THROWING
Four players, four bases. Throw around the horn: home to first, first to second, second to third, third to home.

WHY: Practices accurate throwing AND receiving. Add a timer to make it competitive.

**Slide 5:**
DRILL 4: TEE RELAY RACE
Split into two lines behind a tee. Player hits off the tee, runs to first, tags the next player. First team through wins.

WHY: Hitting reps plus base running. Competitive format keeps energy high.

**Slide 6:**
DRILL 5: PICKLE (RUNDOWN)
Runner caught between two bases. Two fielders try to tag them out. Rotate positions.

WHY: Kids LOVE this one. Teaches base running instincts and fielding communication. End practice with this and they'll beg to come back.

### Post 2: "First Practice Help"

Your first practice is tomorrow. You have no plan. Here's what to do.

I've been there. Every coach has been there. And I'm going to save you about two hours of panicked Googling.

The 60-minute template that works for any sport:

Warm-up (10 min): Jog, stretch, partner activity. Get the wiggles out. This is non-negotiable.

Skill work (25 min): Pick ONE skill. Not three, not five. One. Run two or three short drills focused on it. Keep each drill under 8 minutes. When they start losing focus, switch.

Scrimmage (20 min): Let them play. This is the fun part. This is why they signed up. Step back and let them figure things out.

Huddle (5 min): Tell them one thing the team did well. Ask them what was fun. End on a high note.

That's the whole thing. No secret coaching knowledge required. Just show up, be positive, and keep them moving.

### Post 4: "Practice Template Share"

I keep getting asked how I plan my practices, so here it is. This is the exact template I use every single week, and it works for any sport.

I call it the 10-25-20-5 template:

10 minutes: Warm-up. Same routine every week. Jog, stretch, partner activity. The kids know what to expect, and you save yourself planning time.

25 minutes: Skill work. Pick one or two fundamentals. Run short drills (5-8 minutes each). When energy drops, switch the drill. More reps, less talking.

20 minutes: Scrimmage or game play. This is their reward for working hard. Let them play. Coach from the sideline, not the middle of the field.

5 minutes: Team huddle. One thing they did well as a team. One thing to work on. "Great practice today. See you Thursday."

That's 60 minutes. Clean, simple, repeatable.

The key is that you don't need to reinvent the wheel every week. Same structure, different skills. The kids get the routine, you get your sanity, and practice runs itself.

### Post 5: "The Tough Kid Advice Post"

Okay, real talk. Let's talk about the kid who's making your season really hard.

Every team has one. The kid who won't listen. The kid who distracts everyone during drills. The kid whose behavior makes you question why you volunteered for this.

I've coached that kid. Multiple times. And here's what I've learned.

That kid is almost never trying to make your life difficult. They're bored (your drill might be too long). They're overwhelmed (too many instructions at once). Or they're dealing with something at home that has nothing to do with softball or soccer.

What actually works:

Give them responsibility. "Hey, you're my cone captain today. Set up the next drill for me." Kids who feel important act important.

Shorten your drills. If a kid is acting out at minute six of a drill, the drill needed to end at minute five. Shorter activities, faster transitions, more movement.

Catch them being good. The first time they do something right, call it out. Loud. In front of everyone. They will spend the rest of practice trying to earn that again.

Stand close, talk quiet. Don't yell corrections from across the field. Walk over, stand next to them, and say it calmly. Proximity beats volume every time.

And finally: give yourself grace. You are a volunteer. You are not a child psychologist, a special education teacher, or a behavioral therapist. You're a parent who showed up. Do your best and know that your best is enough.

CoachPilot won't solve the behavior stuff (I wish), but it will give you a solid practice plan so you have one less thing to worry about. coachpilot.org/plans
