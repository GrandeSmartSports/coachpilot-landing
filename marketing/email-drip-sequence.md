## Email 2: The #1 Mistake (Sent Day 2)

**Subject:** The mistake every new coach makes (I made it too)
**Preview text:** It's not what you think.

---

Hey Coach,

The #1 mistake first-year coaches make?

Trying to teach too much in one practice.

I've done it. Spent 45 minutes on a whiteboard explaining defensive rotations to a group of 8-year-olds who just wanted to hit the ball. Their eyes glazed over by minute 3.

Here's what works: **one skill per practice.**

Pick ONE thing: throwing, dribbling, serving, whatever. Build the whole practice around it. Warmup reinforces it. Drills practice it. Scrimmage uses it.

By the end, every kid has improved at that one thing. That's a good practice.

Tomorrow I'll share the simplest practice structure that works for any sport.

Coach Daniel

## Email 3: The Template (Sent Day 3)

**Subject:** The only practice template you need
**Preview text:** Works for any sport, any age group.

---

Hey Coach,

Every good practice follows the same structure. Doesn't matter if it's softball, soccer, basketball, or volleyball. Here it is:

**The 60-Minute Template:**

1. **Warm-Up (10 min):** Jog, dynamic stretches, light version of today's skill
2. **Skill Work (25 min):** 2-3 drills focused on one skill
3. **Game Play (20 min):** Scrimmage or competitive game using that skill
4. **Cool Down (5 min):** Stretch, team huddle, one positive takeaway

That's it. Memorize this and you can run a practice for any sport on zero notice.

The time splits adjust for younger kids (shorter skill work, more games) and older kids (more skill work, more complex scrimmage). But the structure stays the same.

## Email 4: The Tough Kid (Sent Day 4)

**Subject:** What to do about THAT kid
**Preview text:** Every team has one. Here's what actually works.

---

Hey Coach,

Every team has one. The kid who won't listen. Who tackles during non-contact drills. Who makes the other kids frustrated.

Before you label them a problem, try this:

**Give them a job.**

"Hey, can you be my assistant coach for this drill? I need you to show the younger kids how to hold the bat."

Nine times out of ten, the "problem kid" is the one with the most energy and the least to do. Give them responsibility and watch what happens.

Other tricks that work:
- **Stand near them** during instructions (proximity = attention)
- **Catch them being good.** "I love how you were first in line. That's leadership."
- **Short activities.** If they lose focus in 3 minutes, don't run 10-minute drills
- **Talk to them 1-on-1** after practice. "Hey, I need your help keeping the team focused. Can you do that for me?" Kids rise to expectations.

The hardest kids to coach often become your favorites by the end of the season. I've seen it happen every year.

Tomorrow: the one thing that separates good coaches from great ones.

Coach Daniel
